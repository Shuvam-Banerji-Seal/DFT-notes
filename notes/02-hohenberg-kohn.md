# 02 — Hohenberg–Kohn theorems

> The two Hohenberg–Kohn theorems, proved in 1964, are the **mathematical
> foundation** of density functional theory. They establish that the
> ground-state electron density $\rho(\mathbf{r})$ is, in principle, a
> complete descriptor of a quantum many-body system.

## 2.1 The setup

We work in the **Born–Oppenheimer** picture (see [note 01](01-many-body-qm.html))
with $N$ electrons moving in an external potential $v_\text{ext}(\mathbf{r})$
generated by the nuclei. The electronic Hamiltonian is

$$
\hat H = \hat T + \hat V_{ee} + \sum_i v_\text{ext}(\mathbf{r}_i).
$$

The first two operators — kinetic energy $\hat T$ and electron–electron
interaction $\hat V_{ee}$ — are the *same* for every system. Only
$v_\text{ext}$ varies. In the language of DFT, we say that $\hat T$ and
$\hat V_{ee}$ together define a **universal** Hamiltonian $\hat F = \hat T + \hat V_{ee}$.

## 2.2 Hohenberg–Kohn I: density uniquely determines the potential

<div class="note-box idea">
<strong>Theorem (HK I, 1964).</strong> For any system of $N$ interacting
electrons in an external potential $v_\text{ext}(\mathbf{r})$, the
ground-state electron density $\rho(\mathbf{r})$ uniquely determines
$v_\text{ext}(\mathbf{r})$ (up to an additive constant).
</div>

The proof is by contradiction. Suppose two *different* potentials
$v_\text{ext}$ and $v'_\text{ext}$ — differing by more than a constant — give
rise to the *same* ground-state density $\rho(\mathbf{r})$. They have
different Hamiltonians $\hat H$ and $\hat H'$ with different ground states
$\Psi$ and $\Psi'$ and ground-state energies $E_0$ and $E_0'$.

By the Rayleigh–Ritz variational principle applied to the *wrong* Hamiltonian,

$$
E_0 = \langle \Psi | \hat H | \Psi \rangle
    < \langle \Psi' | \hat H | \Psi' \rangle
    = \langle \Psi' | \hat H' | \Psi' \rangle + \int \bigl(v_\text{ext} - v'_\text{ext}\bigr)\,\rho(\mathbf{r})\,d^3r,
$$

so

$$
E_0 < E_0' + \int \bigl(v_\text{ext} - v'_\text{ext}\bigr)\,\rho(\mathbf{r})\,d^3r .
$$

Repeating the argument with $\hat H \leftrightarrow \hat H'$ and adding the
two inequalities gives the contradiction $E_0 + E_0' < E_0' + E_0$. Hence
no such pair of distinct potentials can exist. $\blacksquare$

> **Consequence.** The mapping $v_\text{ext} \mapsto \rho$ is invertible (for
> non-degenerate ground states). Therefore $\rho$ determines $v_\text{ext}$,
> which determines $\hat H$, which determines *every property* of the system
> — in principle. We just need a way to *use* $\rho$ to compute observables.

## 2.3 Hohenberg–Kohn II: a variational principle

<div class="note-box idea">
<strong>Theorem (HK II).</strong> A universal energy functional
$F_\text{HK}[\rho]$ exists, defined on densities that are $v$-representable,
such that for any such density
</div>

$$
E_0 = \min_{\rho}\Bigl\{F_\text{HK}[\rho] + \int v_\text{ext}(\mathbf{r})\,\rho(\mathbf{r})\,d^3r\Bigr\},
$$

and the minimum is attained at the true ground-state density.

The functional is *universal* in the sense that $F_\text{HK}$ depends only on
$N$ — i.e. on $\hat T$ and $\hat V_{ee}$ — and is the same for *every*
external potential.

> **What $F_\text{HK}$ is.** HK II doesn't tell us the form of
> $F_\text{HK}[\rho]$. It just guarantees it exists. The whole art of practical
> DFT is designing *approximations* to $F_\text{HK}$.

## 2.4 What "v-representable" means

A density $\rho(\mathbf{r})$ is **$v$-representable** if it is the
ground-state density of *some* external potential $v(\mathbf{r})$. The HK
theorems, in their original form, only cover such densities.

<div class="note-box warn">
<strong>Subtlety.</strong> Not every nice non-negative $\rho$ with the right
integral is $v$-representable. The Levy–Lieb constrained-search formulation
(<em>Phys. Rev. A</em> 26, 2124; <em>Int. J. Quantum Chem.</em> 24, 243) replaces
$v$-representability with a weaker condition and shows the universal
functional is well-defined as
</div>

$$
F[\rho] = \min_{\Psi \to \rho} \langle \Psi | \hat T + \hat V_{ee} | \Psi \rangle,
$$

where the minimization is over *all* antisymmetric $\Psi$ that yield the
given $\rho$ — not just ground states. This is the form most modern proofs
and implementations use.

## 2.5 What HK does and does not give you

| HK gives you | HK does *not* give you |
| --- | --- |
| A *definition* of $F[\rho]$ | The *form* of $F[\rho]$ |
| A *variational principle* on the 3D space of densities | A *practical algorithm* |
| A proof that $\rho$ is enough in principle | A way to *find* $\rho$ for any system |
| A guarantee that $E_0 = \min F + \int v\rho$ | A *recipe* for the kinetic energy of an interacting system |

The missing pieces are filled in by Kohn–Sham — next note.

## Problems

1. **Constrained search.** Show that for a one-electron system, $F[\rho] =
   T_s[\rho]$ (the non-interacting kinetic energy), with no electron–electron term.
2. **The HK I mapping is a one-line proof that the density uniquely determines
   the dipole moment of a finite system.** Prove it.
3. **Spin DFT.** The HK mapping fails for spin densities of the form
   $\rho_\uparrow = \rho_\downarrow$. Why? (Hint: any two densities satisfying
   this are interchangeable by a constant spin flip.)
